Section I
The Hook

March 25, 2026

On March 25, 2026, Pop Mart International reported the best financial year in its history. Revenue reached approximately $5.38 billion USD, a 184.7% increase over the prior year.1 Net profit surged 308%.1 The company had grown faster than almost any consumer brand of comparable scale anywhere in the world.

By the close of trading that week, Pop Mart's shares had fallen 31%.

The market was not misreading the numbers. It was reading what they contained. The Labubu character had generated approximately 40% of the company's total global revenue in 2025, up from 23% the year before.1 Inventory turnover had risen to 123 days — for trend-driven goods, a signal that demand is weakening.1 Management's forward guidance projected growth of just over 20% for 2026, a deceleration from 185% that the market received as a confession.1

The stock's collapse wiped approximately $33 billion USD from the company's total share value over five trading sessions. It was not a verdict on the past. It was a question about the future.

Six days before the earnings report, Pop Mart had offered its own answer. On March 19, 2026, the company and Sony Pictures confirmed that Paul King — the British filmmaker behind Paddington, Paddington 2, and Wonka — would direct a Labubu feature film.2 Steven Levenson, the Tony Award-winning writer of Dear Evan Hansen, was named as co-writer.2 Kasing Lung, the Hong Kong-born illustrator who created Labubu, was attached as executive producer.2

Wang Ning had spent the previous five years arguing, in public and in press, that IP did not need a story to succeed. The film announcement represented the moment at which the company's governing philosophy could no longer be sustained.

Section II
The Context

2010 – 2016

Wang Ning did not set out to build a company whose business model rested on characters deliberately stripped of story. He set out to build something like LOG-ON, the Hong Kong retail chain he encountered in 2010. LOG-ON mixed toys, cosmetics, and stationery in a format that felt unlike any retail he had seen in Beijing. He returned and opened Pop Mart, initially a general-format lifestyle store that sold, among other things, imported collectibles.

The pivot to character IP came not from philosophy but from data. In 2015, Wang Ning noticed that Sonny Angel — a small palm-sized figurine of a cherubic child with angel wings produced in dozens of themed variations and sold as a series — was generating approximately 30% of Pop Mart's total revenue from a single display in one of his Beijing stores.4 He approached the Japanese distributor about an exclusive arrangement. The response was a refusal.

The dependence on a single licensed character Pop Mart did not own nearly ended the company. Wang Ning needed an immediate replacement: visually distinctive, legally owned, requiring no deep narrative development. In 2016, he flew to Hong Kong to sign Kenny Wong, a designer whose Molly character — a wide-eyed girl with a pursed expression — had been selling in limited editions in the designer toy market.4 Molly had no backstory, no officially established personality, no official history. Wang Ning did not consider this a deficiency. He considered it a feature.

The "no story needed" philosophy was publicly codified between 2020 and 2021. Wang Ning's formulation, as recorded by Jiemian News in 2020, was that "IP doesn't need a story because everyone can project their own story onto it."5 The formulation was not dishonest. But it also rationalized, with considerable elegance, an organizational constraint: Pop Mart had no narrative production capability at all.

Somewhere in that arc, the absence of story became a philosophy.

Wang Ning had not designed a company around the principle that characters should have no stories. He had signed a character that happened to have no story and watched it succeed. Then he reasoned backward to a philosophy that explained it.

The less a character meant, the more it could mean.

When 36Kr reported in early 2026 that Pop Mart's internal animation effort had "ended without results,"6 the finding confirmed what the organizational chart had always implied: the company was a product company, not a storytelling studio, and the distinction was organizational, not temporary.

By 2025, Pop Mart's portfolio had expanded well beyond Molly. Six character families each generated more than $275 million in annual revenue, led by SKULLPANDA and CRYBABY — a teardrop-faced figure that had become a breakout hit across Asia.4 Each character was visually distinctive but narratively empty.

The company operated more than 630 retail stores and 2,600 robotic vending machines across dozens of countries. Gross margins exceeded 72%.1 It was a high-margin consumer business whose founding philosophy had been born from a licensing crisis, rationalized after the fact, and never tested against the question it would eventually have to answer: what happens when blankness is not enough?

Section III
The Move

March 19, 2026

Pop Mart's internal animation effort failed. That failure is what the Sony/Paul King announcement was built to address, and what makes it strategy rather than entertainment.

The choice of King is worth examining in detail. His three completed features are Paddington (2014), Paddington 2 (2017), and Wonka (2023). Each was built on IP carrying decades of accumulated audience expectation. The belonging anxiety that drives the first Paddington film is not in Bond's novels. King located it inside the IP, in latent form, and built the film around it. He then deepened it in the sequel: Paddington 2 is about goodness being punished by a cynical world. Wonka found a different character inside Roald Dahl's material: a young man who believes in the impossible and chooses to believe anyway despite the world punishing him for it.

The pattern is consistent. King does not adapt source material. He interrogates it for the wound — the character's central unresolved emotional problem the source material has not yet named — and builds the film around healing that wound.

Kasing Lung's picture books contain the seed. Labubu is a forest creature who is mischievous but kind-hearted, capable of unintentional harm, permanently hovering between monster and friend. The character wants to belong and keeps failing to belong. The gap between intention and effect is already there. King's task is activation, not invention.

What is not confirmed is the governance of the creative process. It is unknown what creative approval rights Pop Mart retains over the script or the emotional proposition King constructs. Pop Mart's COO Si De stated in 2025 that "movies are not Pop Mart's goal" — a framing that suggests the company views the film primarily as a commercial event built around an existing brand rather than as a commitment to building a narrative franchise. The announcement leaves that question open.

Pop Mart's design pipeline moves a character from initial sketch to finished product on a timeline most toy companies cannot match. The process is modular: a character's base sculpt is developed as a three-dimensional mold, then multiplied across color variations, accessories, and seasonal themes. New series can reach shelves within weeks of concept approval.

But the pipeline produces objects. Every stage is concerned with how a character looks, not what it is. Pop Mart's Chief Marketing Officer described the company's brand strategy in terms of four pillars: product design, retail experience, community engagement, and global expansion. None of them is narrative.

Section IV
The Mechanism

April 2024

Pop Mart's actual competitive advantage sits beneath the blind box format and the collectible scarcity: the company's demonstrated ability to export a feeling without explaining it.

Pop Mart succeeded in the United States not because Labubu is culturally neutral but because the company engineered a specific kind of cultural decoupling. Consumers in the United States who purchased Labubu in 2024 and 2025 did not do so because they understood or valued Labubu's cultural origins. Field research conducted outside Pop Mart's Manhattan flagship store found no evidence of the Guochao hypothesis — that US consumers were purchasing Labubu as an active expression of engagement with Chinese culture specifically. What the interviews found instead was an access-as-status dynamic: Labubu as an affordable entry point into the kind of consumption associated with taste and cultural awareness.

This is a contemporary version of the Lipstick Effect: the pattern by which affordable luxury items capture spending when larger luxuries are out of reach. Pop Mart did not create a luxury object. It created an accessible luxury display, priced at a point where the cost of acquisition was low enough to permit impulsive purchase and high enough to register as a considered one.

What Pop Mart has never needed to determine is which type of consumer is buying — the mechanic-loyal keeper (drawn by the variable reward of the blind box) or the character-loyal keeper (with a specific emotional relationship with Labubu). The figure worked for both. The feeling was delivered without requiring that the company understand what, precisely, the consumer was feeling.

A film cannot operate on that basis. A film must commit to a specific emotional proposition and hold it for ninety minutes.

The audience that arrived with the Labubu surge was new to Pop Mart. Before the April 2024 photograph, the customer base had been predominantly male and concentrated among Chinese collectors. Within a year, global data indicated that more than 60% of buyers were now female, skewing younger and spreading well beyond Asia.17 Southeast Asia, Europe, and North America became significant markets for the first time.

The speed of the transformation obscured something important about its nature. Labubu's breakout was not a product launch. Pop Mart did not engineer it, market it, or anticipate it. The audience it unlocked had no prior relationship with blind-box collecting, designer toys, or Pop Mart's existing brand identity. They were buying a plush accessory. The format was different, the customer was different, the purchase behavior was different, and the company had not changed with them.

Section V
The Evidence

2024 – 2026

Two facts establish the boundaries of what is known about Pop Mart's US market position.

The first is that the cultural foundation has been built. By the close of 2025, Pop Mart had opened flagship stores in New York, Los Angeles, Las Vegas, and Chicago. On November 27, 2025, Pop Mart debuted its first float in the Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade.11 In March 2026, the company announced a licensing collaboration with FIFA ahead of the World Cup.12 Both moves represent a transition from viral subcultural moment to institutionalized mainstream presence.

The second fact is that the cultural momentum preceding this investment has peaked and begun to decline. Google Trends data for "Labubu" in the United States shows a sharp peak in mid-2025, followed by a decline through the remainder of the year.13 The investment and the momentum are moving in opposite directions.

Consumer Edge transaction data show that transaction frequency per US user fell in each successive measured period through 2025, with first-time buyer volume contracting across the same window.14 The blind box and the plush fashion accessory serve different consumer needs. A consumer who buys a Labubu plush charm for a handbag completes a fashion transaction. Twelve variations of the charm will not bring her back. The uncertainty-and-reward cycle that drives blind box repeat purchase does not apply to the fashion category.

Pop Mart acquired millions of US consumers during the peak of Labubu's cultural heat. The 2025 results surface the question the 2026 forecast does not answer: whether those consumers will remain engaged once the initial acquisition impulse has been satisfied.

One character now accounted for more than a third of all revenue. That character had a creator with a narrative vision the company had chosen not to use. And the product format that carried it had replaced the mechanical engine that had driven Pop Mart's recurring revenue since its founding.

Plush toys, which had accounted for 9.8% of revenue in 2023, reached 50.4% by the end of 2025. The Monsters IP alone generated $1.95 billion — more than the next five character families combined.1

Interlude
The Beanie Baby and the Blank Face

1993 – 1999

Beanie Babies peaked at approximately $1.4 billion in annual revenue in 1999 and left no lasting franchise. Hello Kitty has generated an estimated $80 billion in lifetime retail revenue across fifty years. Both ran on the same blankness. The difference was organizational.

Ty Warner managed supply by periodically retiring specific models, using artificial scarcity to drive a secondary market in which individual animals traded for hundreds of dollars. When Warner decided to capture the demand that scarcity had created, he flooded the market. The secondary market collapsed. By 2001, the cultural moment had passed entirely.

1974 – present

Hello Kitty, created by Sanrio in 1974, has no mouth, no official backstory, and no official personality beyond the descriptor "kind." Sanrio commissioned a young designer named Yuko Shimizu to create a character for a line of coin purses. Shimizu has said she did not draw a mouth because she wanted the character's expression to reflect the viewer's mood.

What Sanrio built, over five decades, was a licensing network of extraordinary breadth: more than 1,700 active licensees across 109 countries. The blankness works because the network makes the character inescapable. Ty Warner built inventory. Sanrio built permanence.

The parallel to Pop Mart is uncomfortably precise. Pop Mart's production increase from 300,000 plush units per month to an estimated 30 million per month in under two years is, in its commercial logic, closer to Ty Warner's decision to flood the market than to Sanrio's patient expansion. The company's revenue concentration in a single character, the declining first-time buyer rate, and the erosion of resale premiums are the early indicators of the same curve Beanie Babies traced.

The film, if it works, is the intervention that could bend the trajectory from Ty Warner's path toward Sanrio's. If it does not work, the inventory has already been built.

Section VI
The Limits

Four Material Risks

The concentration risk is most immediately visible. In 2025, Labubu and the Monsters series generated approximately 40% of Pop Mart's total global revenue. No other IP is within range. The company's development process for new characters has not demonstrated the capacity to generate a successor at comparable scale.

The tariff exposure affects day-to-day costs. Pop Mart manufactures in China and exports to the United States under conditions that have become substantially more expensive since 2024. The company implemented US price increases of 80–100% in mid-2024. If the price increases beyond the "indulgent but not consequential" threshold, the access-as-status dynamic is disrupted.

The fad cycle risk is the one the market is pricing most directly. Pop Mart's COO Si De described Labubu as riding a "seven-year tidal wave." Wang Ning's stated supply philosophy — keep the market "seven-tenths full" — acknowledges the tension between growth and scarcity preservation. It does not resolve it.

The cultural translation risk is the most relevant to the film. Pop Mart succeeded in the United States by bypassing cultural context entirely. A film asks something different. Pew Research Center's 2025 survey found 77% of US respondents holding unfavorable views of China — a measurement of broad public opinion that did not prevent Labubu from reaching mainstream US retail presence, but one that registers differently when a film carries explicit Chinese cultural authorship.18

Whether Paul King's approach to Labubu will produce an emotional proposition that works for a US audience that acquired the brand as a fashion object, without any prior relationship with Kasing Lung's picture books, is not knowable in advance. Section VII establishes the odds. It cannot establish the outcome.

Section VII
The Pattern

The Analysis

The films that work follow a three-step pattern. The audience comes for the brand. The audience stays for the wound. And the audience credits the brand for the healing — not the craft of the filmmaker, but the character itself. The IP absorbs the credit. It becomes, in the audience's memory, the thing that understood something true about their life.

This study examined 35 films released between 1985 and 2024 in which the primary IP originated as a physical object and the film received a wide theatrical release. The primary corpus was confined to companion-character IP — properties designed around an emotional relationship with an individual owner or viewer. Action-figure IP and participatory-world franchises operate on different economics and are analyzed separately.

The standard assumption — that prior narrative material is the key variable — is not supported by the data. Across the companion-IP films in the corpus, the correlation between prior narrative richness and commercial returns was a coefficient of -0.06 with a p-value of 0.72. No meaningful relationship at all. Having more story behind a character predicts neither success nor failure.

For each film in the primary corpus, this analysis applied a single binary test: can the central emotional conflict be restated in a sentence without naming the IP?

The LEGO Movie's controlling idea: a father learns that controlling a creative child is a form of withheld love. That idea requires no LEGO. Barbie's controlling idea: the standard women are asked to meet is impossible, and the impossibility is the point. That idea requires no Barbie. Sonic's controlling idea: an outsider hides his true self to avoid rejection and discovers that connection requires exposure. Sonic maintained this across three films, returning 3.8x, 3.7x, and 3.4x — remarkably stable across six years.

Garfield's controlling idea: a cat is lazy and likes lasagna. That describes a personality, not a wound. Wounds can be healed inside a film's running time. Personality traits can only be confirmed. Three films, three studios, three creative teams, across twenty years: 4.0x, 2.4x, 4.3x. The consistency suggests a fixed property of the character rather than a series of production failures.

Films that passed the irreplaceable controlling idea test achieved commercial success (2x return) in 93% of cases in this dataset. Films that failed the test achieved commercial success in 70% of cases. The difference is statistically meaningful (Mann-Whitney U, p = 0.038).

The franchise analysis, which holds IP and budget constant, provides the more reliable evidence. Paddington and Paddington 2, both directed by Paul King with the controlling idea intact, returned 4.9x and 5.7x. Paddington in Peru (2024), directed by a different filmmaker in a structure that moved the character away from the belonging-and-outsider framework, returned 2.35x — commercially viable, but a halving of return on investment within the same franchise at a higher budget with the same core cast.

The credit transfer is why Paddington 2 outperformed Paddington 1 rather than declining, as sequels typically do. The belonging anxiety had deepened. The audience credited the bear. What Pop Mart is deciding is whether Labubu gets to be the thing the audience credits for healing them.

Paul King has directed three films within this analysis's scope: Paddington, Paddington 2, and Wonka. All three pass the irreplaceable controlling idea test. All three returned above 4.5x on production budget. No other director in the corpus has produced three consecutive films with irreplaceable controlling ideas. The average return across the three is 5.2x.

Labubu's central trait is mischief layered over vulnerability: a character who intends kindness and produces harm, who wants to belong and keeps failing to belong. That trait contains an accessible wound. It is, by this analysis, closer to Sonic than to Garfield. King's task is not to invent the wound. It is already in Kasing Lung's picture books. His task is activation.

The determining variable is not in the data. It is whether Pop Mart gives Paul King the latitude that Paddington Books gave Paul King: the latitude to find something true inside Labubu and refuse to subordinate it to brand management. The question, then, is not whether a Labubu film can produce a billion-dollar franchise. The question is whether the company that built a billion-dollar business by distributing a feeling it never needed to understand can, for the first time, afford to understand it.

There is a pattern in consumer brands that is easier to name than to see from the inside. A company encounters a constraint early in its life. Rather than treating the constraint as a problem to solve, the company builds around it. The workaround becomes a position, the position becomes a philosophy, and the philosophy becomes a brand, until no one inside the company can see that it was never a choice.

The philosophy described something real. But it was never the engine. The engine was product design, retail execution, and the behavioral loop of collectibility. The philosophy was an explanation that rested comfortably on top of a business that had never required it to bear weight.

When the engine changed, the philosophy collapsed. The plush pivot replaced the behavioral loop with cultural relevance as the primary driver of repeat purchase. Cultural relevance, unlike a blind-box mechanic, requires a reason. It requires the consumer to feel something specific about the character, not merely to want the next figure in a set. The film is Pop Mart's attempt to supply that reason. Whether it succeeds depends on whether Paul King and Kasing Lung can find inside Labubu the kind of emotional specificity that Lung's original picture books gestured toward but that Pop Mart spent a decade deliberately withholding.

Pop Mart's constraint disappeared the moment the Sony deal was signed. The company now has access to world-class narrative talent, a global distribution partner, and a director whose previous work demonstrates precisely the skill the project requires: finding the emotional core inside a character the world already recognizes but does not yet understand. The question is no longer whether Pop Mart can tell a story. It is whether the company, its investors, and its consumers can survive the transition from a brand that said stories were unnecessary to a brand that is betting its future on one.
