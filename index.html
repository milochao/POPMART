Asia Aisle IP Lab · April 2026

The Feeling Machine

How Pop Mart built a multi-billion-dollar company by distributing a feeling it never needed to understand, and what happens now that understanding has become mandatory.

Asia Aisle
Chapter I

The Hook

On March 25, 2026, Pop Mart International reported the best financial year in its history. Revenue reached RMB 37.12 billion, approximately $5.38 billion USD, a 184.7% increase over the prior year. Net profit attributable to owners surged 308% to RMB 12.78 billion. The company had grown faster than almost any consumer brand of comparable scale anywhere in the world.

By the close of trading that week, Pop Mart's shares had fallen 31%.

The market's reaction was not a misreading of the numbers. It was a reading of what the numbers contained. The Labubu character, part of Pop Mart's Monsters series, had generated approximately 40% of the company's total global revenue in 2025, up from 23% the year before. The company's other flagship properties, Molly and Crybaby among them, had posted results below internal expectations. Inventory turnover had risen to 123 days. Management's forward guidance projected growth of "just over 20%" for 2026, a deceleration from 185% that the market received as a confession.

What the market understood, and what the earnings call confirmed in aggregate, was that Pop Mart's extraordinary performance had been built on a single character's extraordinary moment. That moment showed early signs of passing. Cultural search interest for Labubu, which had peaked in mid-2025, had declined sharply through the back half of the year while the company's infrastructure investment was accelerating. The stock's collapse, which wiped approximately $33 billion USD from the company's market capitalization over five trading sessions, was not a verdict on the past. It was a question about the future.

Pop Mart responded ten days later. On March 19, 2026, the company and Sony Pictures jointly confirmed that Paul King, the British filmmaker behind Paddington, Paddington 2, and Wonka, had been attached to direct a Labubu feature film. Steven Levenson, the Tony Award-winning writer of Dear Evan Hansen, was named as co-writer. Kasing Lung, the Hong Kong-born illustrator who created Labubu in 2006, was attached as executive producer. The film would be a hybrid of live-action and computer-generated imagery, the same format King had used for the Paddington franchise. It would feature, Wang Ning confirmed in a statement to Xinhua, "a very warm and touching story" in which Labubu enters the human world.

Wang Ning had spent the previous five years arguing, in public and in press, that IP did not need a story to succeed. The film announcement represented something more specific than a change of mind. It represented the moment at which the company's governing philosophy had run out of runway.

Chapter II

The Context

Wang Ning did not set out to build a blank-canvas IP company. He set out to build something like LOG-ON, the Hong Kong retail chain he encountered in 2010 while visiting his girlfriend Yang Tao, who was completing her master's degree in the city. LOG-ON mixed toys, cosmetics, and stationery in a format that felt unlike any retail he had seen in Beijing. He returned and opened Pop Mart, initially a general-format lifestyle store that sold, among other things, imported collectibles.

The pivot to character IP came not from philosophy but from data. In 2015, Wang Ning noticed that a Japanese plush toy called Sonny Angel was generating approximately 30% of Pop Mart's total revenue from a small display in one of his Beijing stores. He approached the Japanese distributor about an exclusive arrangement. The response was a refusal. The dependence on a single licensed product that Pop Mart did not own and could not control nearly ended the company. Wang Ning needed an immediate replacement. He needed an IP that was visually distinctive, legally owned, and did not require the kind of deep narrative infrastructure that would take years to build.

In 2016, he flew to Hong Kong to sign Kenny Wong, a designer whose Molly character, a wide-eyed girl with a pursed expression, had been selling in limited art-toy editions. Molly had no backstory, no canonical personality, no official history. Wang Ning did not consider this a deficiency. He considered it, at least in the framing he would later apply to it, a feature.

The "no story needed" philosophy was publicly codified between 2020 and 2021, by which point Pop Mart had listed on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange and begun the process of explaining itself to institutional investors and international press. Wang Ning's formulation, as recorded by Jiemian News in 2020, was that "IP doesn't need a story because everyone can project their own story onto it." The characters were designed as projection surfaces. Blank faces invited interpretation. The absence of canonical narrative was presented as a deliberate choice about the nature of emotional connection.

The formulation was not dishonest. It described something real about how Pop Mart's products worked and why consumers responded to them. But it also rationalized, with considerable elegance, a structural constraint. Pop Mart had no narrative production capability. It had no story development function, no chief narrative officer, no internal creative pipeline for building character mythology. When 36Kr reported in early 2026 that Pop Mart's internal animation effort had "ended without results," the finding confirmed what the organizational chart had always implied: the company was a product company, not a storytelling studio, and the distinction was structural, not temporary.

By March 2026, Wang Ning's framing had shifted. He told CCTV Finance that there was "a preliminary script, still being refined," that "the general story direction has basically been set," and that the film would be "a very warm and touching story." He told Xinhua that the plan was to "build a rich commercial ecosystem encompassing theme parks, products and entertainment." The philosophy that had governed the company for the better part of a decade was not abandoned in the announcement. It was superseded by the acknowledgment that the philosophy's conditions no longer applied.

Chapter III

The Move

The Sony/Paul King announcement is not primarily a film announcement. It is a diagnosis, communicated through corporate action, of the limits of the company's existing model.

Pop Mart's internal animation effort failed. The company required an external partner with the capability to produce the narrative it could not manufacture internally. Sony Pictures provided distribution infrastructure and industry credibility. Paul King provided something more specific: a documented track record of taking IP with a fixed audience expectation and finding, inside that IP, an emotional argument that had not yet been articulated.

The choice of King is worth examining precisely. His three completed features are Paddington (2014), Paddington 2 (2017), and Wonka (2023). Each was built on IP carrying decades of accumulated audience expectation. Michael Bond's Paddington is a confident, competent bear who navigates London with cheerful practicality. King's Paddington is an outsider whose goodness is genuinely tested by a world that has forgotten how to welcome strangers. The belonging anxiety that drives the first film is not in Bond's novels. King located it inside the IP, in latent form, and built the film's architecture around it. He then deepened it in the sequel: Paddington 2 is about goodness being punished by a cynical world, and community restoring what institutions cannot. The film holds the highest critical approval rating of any family film released in the decade in which it appeared.

Wonka (2023) demonstrates the same capability applied to different source material. Roald Dahl's Willy Wonka is eccentric and slightly menacing. King found a different character inside the same IP: a young man who believes in the impossible and discovers that the world will punish him for it, then chooses to believe anyway. The film returned 5.1x on its production budget.

The pattern is consistent and specific. King does not adapt source material. He interrogates it for the wound the source material has not yet named, and then builds the film around the act of healing that wound. His job on Labubu is not categorically different. Kasing Lung's picture books, which introduced Labubu as a forest creature who is mischievous but kind-hearted, capable of unintentional harm, permanently hovering between monster and friend, contain the seed. The character wants to belong and keeps failing to belong. The gap between intention and effect is already there. King's task is activation, not invention.

What is not confirmed, and what the announcement does not address, is the governance structure of the creative process. It is unknown whether Pop Mart is co-financing the production or licensing the IP for a standard royalty. It is unknown what creative approval rights Pop Mart retains over the script, the character's depiction, or the emotional argument King constructs. COO Si De's statement, recorded in a 2025 industry interview, that "movies are not Pop Mart's goal" suggests a company that views the film primarily as IP activation rather than narrative investment. Whether that orientation is compatible with the kind of film King has demonstrated the capacity to make is the question the announcement raises without answering.

Chapter IV

The Mechanism

Pop Mart's actual competitive advantage is not the blind box. It is not the collectible format or the scarcity mechanic or the retail store experience. These are delivery systems. The competitive advantage is the company's demonstrated ability to export a feeling without explaining it.

The working hypothesis, refined through adversarial challenge with China media analyst Manya Koetse, is that Pop Mart succeeded in the United States not because Labubu is culturally neutral but because the company engineered a specific kind of cultural decoupling. The characters are not culturally stateless. The "ugly-cute" aesthetic that defines Labubu, the deliberate tension between the unsettling and the endearing, carries specific references to Hong Kong artist culture, to Kasing Lung's particular biography, to the visual vocabulary of independent art toys that developed in East Asia from the early 2000s. None of that context is required to form an emotional attachment to the object. The decoupling is the product.

Consumers in the United States who purchased Labubu in 2024 and 2025 did not do so because they understood or valued Labubu's cultural origins. Field research conducted outside Pop Mart's Manhattan flagship store found no consumer-generated evidence of what might be called the Guochao hypothesis: the idea that US consumers were purchasing Labubu as an expression of engagement with Chinese culture specifically. What the consumer interviews found was an access-as-status dynamic: Labubu as a precision-calibrated entry point to aspirational consumption. At $22 to $28 for a standard plush charm, the product is indulgent but not financially consequential. Attached to a handbag worth multiples of its price, it functions as a signal of taste without requiring the full investment that taste typically demands.

This is the Lipstick Effect in a specific contemporary form. Pop Mart did not create a luxury product. It created an accessible luxury signal, priced at a point where the cost of acquisition was low enough to permit impulsive purchase and high enough to register as a considered one. The blind box mechanic introduced a variable reward layer: the neurochemical pattern of the sealed box, the cascade of anticipation and uncertainty, is the same architecture that governs slot machines and social media feeds. Pop Mart built a slot machine that generated a fashion accessory as its prize.

The consumer segments that emerged from this product design are two, not three. Displayers are consumers who wear or display Labubu as a social signal. The product is a bag charm, a desk object, a marker of membership in a specific aesthetic community. Keepers are consumers who collect with deeper attachment, whose relationship with specific characters or series goes beyond display. Within the Keeper segment, there is a critical distinction that the available data cannot resolve: whether the attachment is mechanic-loyal (the variable reward of the blind box is the product) or character-loyal (the specific emotional relationship with Labubu is the product). This distinction matters for the film. A mechanic-loyal Keeper will not become the audience that credits Labubu for a self-recognition experience. A character-loyal Keeper might.

What Pop Mart has never needed to determine is which type of consumer is buying. The product worked for both. The feeling was delivered without requiring that the company understand what, precisely, the consumer was feeling. The film ends that arrangement.

Chapter V

The Evidence

Two facts establish the boundaries of what is known about Pop Mart's US market position.

The first is that the cultural infrastructure has been built. By the close of 2025, Pop Mart had opened flagship stores in New York, Los Angeles, Las Vegas, and Chicago. On November 27, 2025, Pop Mart debuted its first float in the Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade, featuring Labubu and other flagship characters at scale in the most traditional family-oriented cultural event in the United States. In March 2026, the company announced a licensing collaboration with FIFA ahead of the World Cup hosted across the United States, Mexico, and Canada. Both moves represent a transition from viral subcultural moment to institutionalized mainstream presence. They are the kind of placements that take years to negotiate and that no amount of TikTok momentum can shortcut.

The second is that the cultural momentum that preceded this infrastructure investment has peaked and declined. Google Trends data for "Labubu" in the United States shows a sharp peak in mid-2025, followed by a decline through the remainder of the year that continued into early 2026. The infrastructure and the momentum are moving in opposite directions. Pop Mart is building permanent retail presence for a brand whose viral energy, as measured by organic consumer interest, is contracting. Whether the infrastructure can generate durable demand after the initial cultural moment has passed is the operational question the company's 2026 guidance declines to answer.

The consumer data from the US market reveals a related structural problem. Transaction frequency per US user is declining sequentially. The influx of first-time buyers is contracting. Meanwhile, the product mix is shifting heavily toward plush: plush toy revenue reached RMB 18.7 billion in 2025, a 560.6% year-over-year increase, representing 50.4% of total global revenue. The blind box mechanic and the plush fashion accessory serve different consumer needs and generate different repeat purchase economics. A consumer who buys a Labubu plush charm for a handbag completes a fashion transaction. She does not require twelve variations of the charm to achieve satisfaction. The variable reward schedule that drives the blind box's repeat purchase rate does not apply to the fashion category. The LTV model that worked for blind box collectibles does not transfer to bag charms.

Pop Mart acquired millions of US consumers during the peak of Labubu's cultural heat. The question that the 2025 results surface, and that the 2026 guidance does not resolve, is whether those consumers will remain engaged once the initial acquisition impulse has been satisfied.

Chapter VI

The Limits

There are four structural risks in Pop Mart's US trajectory that the film announcement does not address.

The concentration risk is the most immediately visible. In 2025, Labubu and the Monsters series generated approximately 40% of Pop Mart's total global revenue. No other IP in the portfolio is within range of that figure. Molly, the character that originally validated the blank-canvas IP model, posted below-expectation results. The company's IP pipeline has not demonstrated the capacity to generate a successor to Labubu at comparable scale. When the market sold off after the earnings announcement, it was pricing this concentration. The film, if it succeeds on the terms described in Section VII of this analysis, could extend Labubu's cultural lifecycle by several years. If it does not succeed on those terms, it validates the concentration risk without resolving it.

The tariff exposure is operational rather than strategic but materially significant. Pop Mart manufactures in China and exports to the United States under conditions that have become substantially more expensive since 2024. The company implemented US price increases of 80-100% in mid-2024, pricing the Labubu 3.0 plush charm at $28 in the United States against RMB 99, approximately $14, in China. That pricing spread reflects a combination of tariff burden, US retail overhead, and brand premium extraction. Under Section 301 List 4A and subsequent executive actions, additional tariff escalation is plausible within the film's production and release window. Price sensitivity in the Lipstick Effect category is by definition limited: if the price increases beyond the "indulgent but not consequential" threshold, the access-as-status mechanism is disrupted. The company has not publicly disclosed a margin floor or a US pricing ceiling.

The fad cycle risk is the one the market is pricing most directly. Pop Mart's COO Si De, in an unguarded moment of strategic disclosure, described Labubu as riding a "seven-year tidal wave." The Beanie Babies comparison, which appeared in analyst reports and media coverage following the earnings selloff, is imprecise in detail but structurally relevant: collectible toys with strong cultural heat, blind box mechanics, and resale market dynamics have historically demonstrated concentrated lifecycle curves. Resale prices for common Labubu editions had already fallen below retail in some markets by late 2025. The supply ramp from approximately 300,000 plush units per month in early 2024 to an estimated 30 million per month by late 2025 had begun to erode the scarcity premium that the brand's early US market penetration was built on. Wang Ning's "seven-tenths full" philosophy, which governs Pop Mart's deliberate supply management, acknowledges the tension between growth and scarcity preservation. It does not resolve it.

The cultural translation risk is the most relevant to the film and the most difficult to quantify. Pop Mart succeeded in the United States without requiring American consumers to know anything about the brand's Chinese origins or to engage with Chinese culture on any terms other than aesthetic ones. The bypassed-culture model worked because the product asked nothing of the consumer's cultural knowledge. A film asks something different. A film requires a filmmaker to make specific choices about character interiority, emotional architecture, and the cultural context in which a wound exists and can be healed. Those choices cannot be made on aesthetics alone. Whether Paul King's approach to Labubu will produce an emotional argument that works for a US audience that acquired the brand as a fashion object, without any prior relationship with Kasing Lung's picture books or the forest-creature mythology that underlies them, is not knowable in advance. What the dataset described in Section VII can establish is the conditional probability, not the outcome.

Chapter VII

The Pattern

Over the past four decades, toy and companion-character IP has produced theatrical films with enough regularity to permit something approaching empirical analysis. This study examined 35 films released between 1989 and 2024 in which the primary IP originated as a physical product, the character or property constituted the film's commercial proposition, and the film received a wide theatrical release. The primary corpus was confined to companion-character IP: properties designed around an emotional relationship with an individual owner or viewer, where the value proposition is the feeling, not spectacle or mythology. Action-figure IP (Transformers, G.I. Joe) and franchise play-space IP (Mario, Pokémon as cultural event) operate on different consumer economics and are analyzed separately.

The standard industry assumption is that prior narrative material is the key variable: that a film based on a character with forty years of comic strips, three animated television series, and a generation of merchandise is better positioned than a film based on a plush toy with no story whatsoever. The data does not support this assumption. Across the companion-IP films in the primary corpus, the correlation between prior narrative richness and commercial gross-to-budget multiple was r = -0.06 (p = 0.72): statistically indistinguishable from zero. Having more story behind a character predicts neither success nor failure.

The Smurfs (2011), built on more than fifty years of Belgian comic strips and multiple animated series, achieved a 5.1x gross-to-budget multiple but was critically dismissed and produced a declining franchise. Paddington, drawn from Michael Bond's fourteen novels, worked. My Little Pony: The Movie, drawn from thirty-five years of Hasbro IP including several television series, failed to find an audience beyond its existing fanbase. The variable that does predict outcomes is different, and considerably harder to manufacture.

The Irreplaceable Argument

For each film in the primary corpus, this analysis applied a single binary test: can the central emotional conflict be restated in a sentence without naming the IP? The question is not whether the film has a plot. It is whether the film's controlling idea is portable , whether it describes a situation that a viewer might recognise from their own life, independent of their relationship with the brand.

The LEGO Movie's controlling idea: a father learns that controlling a creative child is a form of withheld love. That argument requires no LEGO. Barbie's controlling idea: the standard women are asked to meet is impossible, and the impossibility is the point. That argument requires no Barbie. Sonic the Hedgehog's controlling idea: an outsider hides his true self to avoid rejection and discovers that connection requires exposure. That argument requires no Sonic.

Garfield's controlling idea: a cat is lazy and likes lasagna. That argument requires no emotional engagement because it describes a personality, not a wound. The distinction matters. Wounds can be healed inside a film's running time. Personality traits can only be confirmed.

Films that passed the irreplaceable argument test achieved commercial success (gross-to-budget multiple of at least 2x) in 93% of cases. Films that failed the test achieved commercial success in 70% of cases. The difference is statistically meaningful at conventional thresholds (Mann-Whitney U, p = 0.038), with a medium-to-large effect size. A multivariate regression found a positive coefficient for the irreplaceable argument variable (1.40x multiplier) but the model is underpowered at n = 35 to isolate it cleanly from director quality, budget, and franchise effects.¹ The franchise analysis, which holds IP and budget constant across sequels, provides the more reliable evidence.

What the Franchise Pattern Shows

When a franchise maintains its irreplaceable argument, commercial performance is stable. When it loses the argument, performance declines. The pattern holds across every testable franchise in the dataset.

Paddington and Paddington 2, both directed by Paul King with the irreplaceable argument intact, returned 4.9x and 5.7x on production budget respectively. Paddington in Peru (2024), directed by a different filmmaker and with the argument sidelined, returned 1.2x.

The Trolls franchise opened with an irreplaceable argument: Branch has suppressed his grief and learned that authentic joy requires processing real sorrow. Return: 2.8x. Trolls World Tour replaced that argument with a genre war about musical formats. Return: 0.5x theatrical. Trolls Band Together replaced it with a boy-band reunion narrative. Return: 2.2x , commercially viable, but a 21% decline from the original's performance, sustained across three films as the argument remained absent.

The Sonic franchise has maintained its irreplaceable argument across all three films, with Shadow's introduction in the third film extending the core wound rather than replacing it. Return: 3.8x, 3.7x, 3.7x. Stable across six years.

The LEGO franchise offers the most controlled comparison because it held all variables constant while varying the argument across simultaneous releases in 2017. The LEGO Movie (2014): filmmaker-driven, irreplaceable argument present, return 7.8x. The LEGO Batman Movie (2017): irreplaceable argument present (a narcissist discovers that strength requires vulnerability), return 3.9x. The LEGO Ninjago Movie (2017): released the same year, same studio, comparable budget, irreplaceable argument absent. Return: 1.8x. The year and the franchise are identical. The argument is not.

The Exceptions and What They Explain

Two films in the full dataset achieve very high returns without an irreplaceable argument, and both require accounting for.

Pokémon: The First Movie (1998) returned 32.6x on its production budget. The film has no transferable emotional conflict and a 16% critical approval rating. It arrived in US theaters two years after Pokémon became a transnational cultural phenomenon, and its audience was not seeking self-recognition. They were seeking participation in a shared event: collective experience of franchise membership. The consumer mechanism is not emotional resonance but social belonging.

The Super Mario Bros. Movie (2023) returned 13.6x on its production budget with a 96% audience approval rating. Mario contains no accessible emotional wound. The film works because the franchise is not a character with an interior life but a participatory mythology: a play-space that multiple generations have inhabited. The audience score is viewers rating their own memories of playing, not the film's emotional content. These are different consumer mechanisms, and they define the scope of the analysis. The irreplaceable argument predicts outcomes in companion-character IP. It does not apply to franchise play-spaces or participatory cultural events.

The Structural Ceiling

Some characters cannot be SR-activated, regardless of directorial quality or budget. The Garfield franchise has produced three theatrical attempts across twenty years: 2004, 2006, and 2024. The gross-to-budget multiples were approximately 4x, 2.4x, and 4.3x. The critical scores ranged from 15% to 37%. Three different studios. Three different creative teams. The same result, to a degree of consistency that suggests a structural property of the IP rather than a series of production failures.²

Garfield's defining trait is laziness. Laziness is not a wound. No version of Garfield can overcome his laziness and discover connection, because overcoming his laziness would make him a different character. The irreplaceable argument ceiling is structural: the IP provides no accessible wound for a filmmaker to activate.

The Sonic franchise demonstrates the contrast. Sonic's defining trait , speed that creates isolation , is inherently a wound. Isolation can be healed. Three films, all with irreplaceable arguments present, all with returns near 3.7x. The difference between Garfield and Sonic is not execution or budget. It is that one character's central trait is an identity that can only be confirmed and one character's central trait is a problem that can be solved.

Labubu's central trait is mischief layered over vulnerability: a character who intends kindness and produces harm, who wants to belong and keeps failing to belong. That trait contains an accessible wound. It is, by this analysis, closer to Sonic than to Garfield. Whether the wound can survive its activation in a theatrical context is the question the dataset cannot answer.

Paul King as Variable

Paul King has directed three films within this analysis's scope: Paddington, Paddington 2, and Wonka. All three pass the irreplaceable argument test. All three returned above 4.5x on production budget. No other director in the corpus has produced three consecutive irreplaceable-argument films. The average return across the three is 5.2x.

This is not evidence of directorial quality in some general sense. It is evidence of a specific and demonstrable capability: taking an IP with a fixed audience expectation and finding, inside that IP, a controlling idea that the source material had not yet made explicit. Bond's Paddington is a competent, confident bear. King found a different bear inside the same IP, one who carries the anxiety of the outsider whose goodness the world has not yet recognised. He located a wound that the source material contained only in latent form, made it the film's structural centre, and then healed it. His job on Labubu is identical: Kasing Lung's picture books contain the seed, the mischievous forest creature who cannot fully control the effect he has on others. King's task is activation, not invention.

What the Analysis Can and Cannot Predict

The model built from this dataset explains 22% of the variance in commercial returns across companion-IP films. That figure is worth stating plainly. It means that 78% of what determines whether these films succeed is not captured by production budget, prior narrative richness, director track record, franchise installment number, creative origin, or the presence of an irreplaceable argument.³

Given a $150 million production budget and Paul King's demonstrated capability, the comparable cases in this dataset suggest worldwide gross in the range of $800 million to $1 billion if the irreplaceable argument is achieved. Comparable cases without that argument, at similar budget and directorial quality, suggest a range of $600 million to $750 million. Both scenarios constitute commercial success for Pop Mart. The difference between them is the difference between a film that validates the brand and a film that transforms it.

The dataset cannot determine which scenario occurs. The determining variable is not in the data. It is whether Pop Mart gives Paul King the latitude that Paddington Books gave Paul King: the latitude to find something true inside Labubu and refuse to subordinate it to brand management.

Pop Mart built its business by distributing a feeling it never needed to understand. A film cannot do that. A film must commit to a specific emotional argument, hold it for ninety minutes, and hope the audience recognises themselves in it. That is a categorically different kind of production problem from running a blind-box programme or managing a resale market. The prior narrative score finding confirms it: the amount of story a character carries into a film predicts nothing. What predicts outcomes is whether the filmmaker found the wound, and whether they were allowed to heal it.

The commercial mechanism of the highest-performing films in this dataset is worth stating precisely, because it is what separates outlier returns from merely adequate ones. The audience comes for the brand. That is what sells the opening weekend: recognition, anticipation, the accumulated goodwill of whatever relationship the audience already has with the IP. The audience stays for the wound. That is what fills the second act and generates the word-of-mouth that determines whether a film earns its gross in week one or across twelve weeks. And the third step is the one that separates franchise from film: the audience credits the brand for the healing.

Viewers do not leave Barbie attributing their emotional experience to the craft of Greta Gerwig, though the craft is what made the experience possible. They leave attributing it to Barbie. The IP absorbs the credit. It becomes, in the audience's memory, the thing that understood something true about their life. That transference is why The LEGO Movie produced a franchise and The LEGO Ninjago Movie did not, despite sharing a studio, a brand, and a release year. It is why Paddington 2 outperformed Paddington 1 rather than declining, as sequels typically do. The wound had deepened. The audience credited the bear.

What Pop Mart is deciding (the company may not have framed it in these terms, but the decision is structural regardless of how it is framed) is whether Labubu gets to be the thing the audience credits for healing them. That is not a question Paul King can resolve through filmmaking alone. It is a corporate governance question about how much of the character's ambiguity, vulnerability, and interior life Pop Mart is prepared to expose in a theatrical context, knowing that the exposure is precisely what produces the credit transfer. A brand that withholds the wound to protect its image will find that the audience withholds the credit in return. The dataset is consistent on this point across thirty-five films and four decades.

The question, then, is not whether a Labubu film can produce a billion-dollar franchise. The question is whether the company that built a billion-dollar business by distributing a feeling it never needed to understand can, for the first time, afford to understand it.

Methodology Notes

METHODOLOGY NOTES

¹ The irreplaceable argument coding in this analysis was performed by a single analyst, not two independent coders against a formal protocol. A rigorous design would use stratified audience review samples per film, two independent coders, and Cohen's kappa above 0.7 before proceeding. The binary proxy used here is a tractable approximation. Borderline cases required judgment calls. The core findings are unlikely to change under more rigorous coding because the distinction between SR-active and SR-absent films is typically unambiguous. Independent replication is recommended before these findings are treated as conclusive.

² The Garfield consistency is treated here as descriptive, not as a causal claim about the IP's intrinsic ceiling. A rival explanation is that Garfield consistently attracts studio-driven, IP-exercise productions rather than filmmaker-driven ones, and the result reflects creative origin rather than character ceiling. Both explanations may be simultaneously true.

³ The model explains 22% of variance at n = 35 with six predictors. The prediction interval at 95% confidence for a new observation spans approximately plus or minus 1.5 log-ROI units, corresponding to roughly a 4x range in dollar outcomes. The cited figures are central estimates from comparable cases, not reliable point forecasts.

Metric note: gross-to-budget multiple throughout refers to worldwide theatrical gross divided by reported production budget. Marketing costs, exhibitor splits, and home entertainment revenue are excluded. This is standard industry shorthand, not a profit figure.
